How to Write a Remote Job Post Developers Will Actually Read

July 8, 2026 · 6 min read

Transparent pay, real stack, and fewer buzzwords. A short employer checklist for remote developer job posts that convert.

Developers skim. If your post hides salary, stacks buzzwords, or sounds like every other SaaS listing, strong candidates bounce. Here’s what to put on the page.

Lead with role, level, and pay

Put the title, seniority, and salary range in the first screen. “Competitive” is not a range. Include currency and whether equity is offered.

Describe the real stack

List languages, frameworks, cloud, and databases you use daily—not every tool you’ve ever tried. Call out must-haves vs nice-to-haves so mid-level engineers don’t self-reject incorrectly.

Explain remote norms

  • Timezone expectations and core overlap
  • Meeting culture (async-first vs sync-heavy)
  • On-call and incident process
  • Travel requirements (if any)

Show the team and impact

One paragraph on product customers, team size, and what success looks like in 90 days beats five paragraphs of mission poetry.

Drop the filler

Avoid “ninja,” “rockstar,” “fast-paced,” and endless perk lists. Benefits belong in a short bullet block. Discrimination-free language and visa/contractor clarity help globally distributed talent apply with confidence.

Employer checklist

  1. Salary range visible above the fold
  2. Stack and seniority honest
  3. Remote policy specific
  4. Apply path simple (one CTA)
  5. Post kept up to date
